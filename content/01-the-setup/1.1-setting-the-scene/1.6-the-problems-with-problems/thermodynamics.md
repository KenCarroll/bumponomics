# Thermodynamics & Problems

## The Energetic Landscape

The world is not merely a collection of objects; it is a landscape of opportunities and potentials. In the BUMPS philosophy, we recognize that **Human Attention** is the primary force that shapes this landscape. When humans collectively focus on a specific dissonance in their environment—a gap between "what is" and "what could be"—they pool their psychic and physical energy into a single point.

This concentration of focus creates a **BUMP**: a localized spike of high potential energy on the landscape of reality.

A "Problem" is effectively a BUMP. It is not a void; it is a dense, vibrating knot of collective concern. Like a gravity well or a charged particle, this BUMP exerts a force on the humans around it. It creates a field of influence that either **attracts** or **repels**.

## First Law: Conserving the Trouble

The **First Law of Thermodynamics** states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed. The energy constituting a BUMP does not appear out of nowhere. It is drawn from the ecosystem—from the time, money, worry, and labor of the people involved.

Antagonists in our story—often those who profit from the status quo—understand this law intuitively. A unresolved problem is a store of potential energy. As long as the BUMP exists, it holds power. It commands attention. It justifies budgets. These actors therefore try to **maintain the BUMP**. They act like containment fields, working to keep the problem "in place" because the gradient it creates provides them with advantage. They are fighting against the natural flow, adding energy to the system to keep the BUMP localized and potent.

## Entropy and the Drive to Dispel

The **Second Law of Thermodynamics** introduces **Entropy**: the universal tendency for energy to disperse and for systems to move toward equilibrium.

Nature abhors a BUMP. The natural inclination of the universe is to "dispel" this concentrated energy—to flatten the curve. Most humans (the protagonists/solvers) align with this universal drive. They feel the repulsion of the BUMP—the stress, the inefficiency, the friction—and strive to overcome it. They want to solve the problem, releasing that pent-up tension and allowing the energy to flow freely again.

Solving a problem is an act of maximizing entropy in the local context: taking a tight knot of complexity and smoothing it out into a lower-energy state of normal operations.

## Exergy: The Work of Change

**Exergy** is the portion of energy that is available to do useful work. In the tension between the Antagonists (who maintain the BUMP) and the Solvers (who try to dispel it), there is a flow.

When the Solvers succeed, the BUMP is not just destroyed; its energy is transformed. The release of that potential drives progress. The "work" done by solving the problem propels the organization or society forward.

However, the landscape is dynamic. The energy released from flattening one BUMP flows across the landscape and inevitably pools elsewhere. New observations are made. New dissonances are found. The dispersed energy coalesces once again into a **new BUMP**, a new problem to be solved. This is the pulse of progress: a perpetual thermodynamic cycle of accumulation, tension, release, and flow.
