# Economics in a Nutshell: Systems, Beliefs, and the Problem Blindspot

## The Dominant OS: Neoclassical / Market Capitalism
The world's primary economic operating system is built on a few core pillars. While variations exist (from the free-market US model to the state-capitalist Chinese model), the underlying logic remains consistent.
*   **Core Goal:** Growth (measured by GDP). Increasing the total output of goods and services is the proxy for progress.
*   **Key Mechanism:** The Market. Supply and demand intersect to determine prices, which signal scarcity and value.
*   **The Agent:** *Homo Economicus*. A rational, self-interested individual who maximizes utility (happiness/satisfaction) subject to budget constraints.
*   **The Firm:** Maximizes profit for shareholders.
*   **Efficiency:** The holy grail. Allocating resources to their most productive use to minimize waste.

## Running Variants
1.  **Neoliberalism:** Deregulate markets, privatize state assets, reduce trade barriers. Belief that the market is the ultimate information processor and allocator.
2.  **State Capitalism:** The state steers the market to achieve strategic national goals (e.g., China, Singapore). Markets are tools, not masters.
3.  **Social Democracy:** Capitalist engine with a socialist distribution system (e.g., Nordics). High taxes fund robust social safety nets to mitigate market inequality.

## Developing Schools of Thought (The challengers)
As the cracks in the dominant OS widen (inequality, climate crisis), new contenders are emerging:
*   **Ecological Economics:** Taking the biosphere seriously. The economy is a subsystem of the environment, not the other way around. Focus on "Steady State" economies rather than infinite growth.
*   **Doughnut Economics (Kate Raworth):** Aiming for the "sweet spot" between the social foundation (human needs) and the ecological ceiling (planetary boundaries).
*   **Modern Monetary Theory (MMT):** Rethinking money. Governments that issue their own currency cannot run out of money, only resources (inflation is the constraint, not revenue).
*   **Behavioral Economics:** Debunking *Homo Economicus*. Humans are irrational, emotional, and social. Economics must account for psychology.
*   **Mission Economy (Mariana Mazzucato):** Re-imagining capitalism through the lens of public purpose. It argues for "Moonshot" thinking where governments actively shape and create markets (rather than just fixing them) by setting big, bold "Missions" (problems like climate change) that catalyze public-private innovation.
*   **Evolutionary Economics (Nelson & Winter):** Rejects the static equilibrium of standard economics. Instead, it views the economy as a biological ecosystem where firms are "organisms" and their habits/routines are "genes." The market acts as a selection mechanism, weeding out ineffective routines. It aligns with BUMPONOMICS by viewing economic change as a dynamic, path-dependent process of trial, error, and adaptation.
*   **Complexity Economics (Santa Fe Institute):** The closest relative to BUMPONOMICS. It views the economy not as a machine seeking equilibrium, but as a complex adaptive system (like a biosphere). Agents interact, learn, and adapt, creating emergent patterns. It explicitly recognizes "problems" (challenges) as the drivers of evolution and innovation, but it is primarily a *descriptive* science (how the system works) rather than a *normative* playbook (how we should organize to solve specific problems).

## Key Principles & Beliefs (The Dogma)
*   **Scarcity:** Economics is the study of how to allocate scarce resources.
*   **Incentives Matter:** People respond to prices and rewards.
*   **Trade-offs:** There is no such thing as a free lunch. (Opportunity Cost).
*   **Externalities:** Pollution is a "market failure"—an unintended side effect not priced into the transaction.

## The Verdict: Do they focus on Problems?
**Almost... but not quite.**

*   **Complexity & Evolutionary Economics** get tantalizingly close by recognizing that the economy *evolves* through agents solving problems to survive (adaptation).
*   **Mission Economy** gets even closer by explicitly using "Big Problems" (Missions) as the organizing principle for government policy and market shaping.

However, they largely stop short of making "The Problem" the fundamental, granular unit of value and organization for the *entire* economy (not just government missions).

Across the dominant schools (Neoclassical, etc.), "Problems" remain **side effects** or **market failures**.
*   **Demand, not Need:** Markets respond to *effective demand* (ability to pay), not *human need* (problems). A starving person has a huge problem but zero demand in the eyes of the market.
*   **The Invisible Hand is Blind to Problems:** The system assumes that by pursuing self-interest (profit), we accidentally solve problems. But it is often more profitable to *create* problems (addiction, planned obsolescence) or *ignore* them (pollution) than to solve them.
*   **The "bads" are "goods":** If I get cancer and spend $100k on chemo, GDP goes up. If I clean up a river for free, GDP stays flat. The system sees the *expenditure*, not the *resolution* of the problematic situation.

**Conclusion:** Current economic thinking generally treats problems as **externalities** to be managed. Even the most advanced theories like **Mission**, **Evolutionary**, or **Complexity** Economics describe *how* to tackle specific big problems or *how* problems drive evolution, but they don't provide a universal framework for *intentionally organizing* all scales of society around the transformation of BUMPS. There is no explicit "Problem-Based Economics" currently proposed or in operation.
