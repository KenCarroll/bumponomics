# The Catalyst

There is a moment—a "Red Pill" decision point—where you must choose how to perceive reality. Once you take the pill and open the door to this way of thinking, there is no going back. But driving towards that perceived "truth" every day, despite the bumps in the way, takes an enormous amount of energy. It is high risk.

Why take that risk? Why bear the financial, personal, social, and spiritual costs of obsession?

Your position in your environment—your reality, your history, your psychology—determines the exact nature of the problems you perceive. And it determines your specific position in the overarching *Fitness Landscape*.

This book, and the entire Bumponomics framework, is the result of that obsession. It is born from an environmental drive to take action, to move towards a new set of outcomes, and to stop optimizing for solutions when we should be optimizing for problems.

History:
Born 1966 in Southampton UK. 
Mother was unmarried and had to keep the birth secret from her family. 
Mother worked on the production line in a factory making car parts. Initially, she attempted to leave her job, but the HR department, knew how much she enjoyed her job and was suspicious of her motives. She told them she was pregant and they come up with a scheme to help her hide her pregancy from her family. This involved the company telling the parents that she was going to work in a different factory for a few months in Liverpool England. Because in those days having a child out of wedlock was a scandal. And many mothers end up giving up their babies for adoption. 
Mother had a very troubled upbringing. She was burnt as a small child after her dress got caught in the fireplace and she was serverly burnt on her back, arms, and legs. She spent a lot of time in hospital as a child. Born in 1933 to Irish parents she was                                                                                   third youngest in a family of 8. She could not read or write. Her father was quite an agressive man and the whole family lived together in small terraced house in Southampton. During the war years they experienced the Blitz and the bombing of Southampton. Since Southampton was a major port and home to development of the spitfire fighter plane. Southampton was heavily bombed during the war. 

After the war, Southampton was rebuilt and my mother, after a few other jobs, got a job "General Motors" assembly plant in Southampton. She continued to live in the family home with her mother, father, and two youngest brothers

The family experienced many hardships. Were very poor before, during, and after the war. And had many interesting stories from those times. 

After my birth my mother kept me a secret from her family some months. But would visit me regularily whilst I was being cared for by some foster family. At some point, she told my grandmother that she had had a baby. There was a big family meeting and grandfather told her to bring her son home. A friend of the family once told me that he came to the house once as normal, and saw a baby, and asked who's it was. And was told "Don't ask"!  My mother continued working at the factory until she retired aged 65. It was like her 2nd family.

I grew up in Southampton, living in a small terraced house with my grandmother, grandfather, mother, and two uncles. 

The story about my father is complicated. At some point my mother met him, but he didn't seem to want to be with my mother or me. There was another story that he came to the house with a small wooden sailing boat to give to me but was told to keep away from the house. I never had any contact with him and he was rarely mentioned. Instead my mother told me my 5 uncles were my dads! 

My mother was a very strong woman. She had to be. She had a very difficult life. She was very intelligent and had a great sense of humour. She was also very kind and generous. She was a very good mother and grandmother. She passed away in 2023 aged 90.

Life in Southampton was 'interesting'. We lived in a small terraced house in a rough area of Southampton. It was close to the city center and the port. The area was a mix of working class families, and immigrants from all over the world. It became notorious as major red light district in the 1970s and 1980s. Featured on the news and news papers, and was the subject of a TV documentary. It was a very interesting place to grow up. Our next door neighbour was a prostitute and sat in the window. Her name was Maxine. Prositutes would often gather on the corner across the street from our house. My grandmother, by that time as bedridden and lived in a double bed in our front room. She would often watch them from the window and make comments. Violence was a regular event in our lives. Too many stories to describe. I would often find my mother sitting on the stairs with either a iron fire poker or a wooden bat because someone had tried to get into the house. 

I rarely went to school as a child. My grandmother used to like having me at home and my mother also liked having me at home as company for my grandmother. This continue throughout my life. I would stay at home alone, and sometimes go to school for periods of time, but the time varied and was never consistent. Have some school reports that say I was 'bright but unless there was a way to regularily attend school my prospects were poor'. 

I left school at 16 and somehow joined the UK govemenents Youth Training Scheme (YTS) working on scheme with electicians and technicians at Southampton University.

I worked hard and was very enthusiastic. The two electicans I worked with seem to like me and I enjoyed working with them. The scheme involved rotation working in different departments with diffferent people. For example learning wood working with a model builder creating models for testing in a wind tunnel, working in ship science helping setup experienments, sound & vibration research dept on anacohic and reverberation chambers, in the machine shop where I had to build a metal vice from blueprints using lathes and large milling machines, and finally working in the electronics department soldering circuit boards with transistors etc to help build systems to enable researchers conduct experiements. 

It was in the electronics department that I was given a task of stripping down and rebuilding an large audio measurement system from a company called Bruel & Kjaer. I don't recall the exact task. But recall the boss of the electronic dept stressing it was important to follow the instructions and get it right. I had a workbench, safety shoes, and white lab coat. I followed the instructions and everything worked ok. But to my embarrasment had some small parts left over!  I could not work out what I'd done wrong. So I took the time to work after hours to redo the whole thing and figure out what happened. But after redoing the whole thing I still had these small parts left over. I had a choice. It worked fine. I could say nothing and no-one would know. Instead I decided to approach the boss and tell him what happened. I lay the parts on his desk, apologied, and said i could not figure out what went wrong. He looked up and causually said something like "Oh, I put those there!". It was a test he gave all his apprentices. To see if he could trust them. 

Not long after the University announced it was taking on a new set of 'trainees' (apprenctices). There were 5 postions. But they had quite a high requirements in terms of academic qualitications e.g. Good A-levels etc. I had none of those things, although the YTS scheme involved a day release to the local technical collage, there was no way I was going to be able to meet the entry qualifications for the jobs. Despite this I was enourage by the technican's to apply and I did so. Recall having to join a room with lots of applicants and having to sit some exams. I knew I did poorly and didn't expect anything. But to my surprise I was called into a room and informed I had got one of the 5 positions. I know for sure I did not perform well on that exam! 

But they gave me a chance! Recall they said I would need to continue attending collage with an aim to reach an certain level. But that If I found it too hard they would figure something out for me!  That discussion seemed to be a catalyst for me...I recall thinking to myself "They think i'm not able to do it"....and I seemed to take it upon myself to prove to them and myself that I could do it.  I worked hard at work, and in my studies, and performed very well at collage. My job at the university was as a Electronics Technician. Over the next 4 years I continued to develop academically and professionally. Ulimately I outgrew my role and decided the only way to progress was to leave and take a BEng(Hons) degree in Electricl & Electronic engineering at what is now the Northumberland University in Newcastle. 

After graduation I joined British Telecom's famous Research & Development center in Martlesham Ipswich where I worked on the next generation of Network Management Systems. After a few years I left and returned to the south coast where I joined GEC Plessey's Network Management Divisions working on SDH and ATM on large scale software systems developed using Shlear Mellor OOA / OOD techniques and implemented in C++/Unix.

After that I joined NTL's Advanced Product Division developing the early MPEG2 digital encoders and decoders used for the first production digital TV deployments. 

Following that I worked for NEC electonics on developing device driver software for their next generation of multi-media chips. 

After that I joined a consultancy company working for a new telecommmunications operator based in Amsterdam, the Netherlands. They were in the early stages of building a new network across Europe and I was part of the team involved in the design and implementation the complete end-2-end system! Over a period of years I developed in multiple ways, professionally and personally. Learning from being part of a team of multinational people, and regularly working in Amsterdam as a consultant developing video, voice, and data services over IP networks.

Eventually I left the consultancy and joined the operator directly. Living in the Netherlands full time. Where I was given the task of building their new integration labs complex. This was a large data center like facility where all the pan-european video, voice, and data systems would be developed,  integrated, and tested before being deployed across Europe. 

Over many years I was involved in the development of many different systems and services, and I was always learning and developing new skills. For example this period of my life was developing my team building skills and had responsility for developing a large team. 

I also became a father to two beautiful daughters during this period. 

Shortly after the birth of my second daughter I was diagnosed with rare cancer (appendix cancer). This involved a high-risk operation (HIPEC), life changing surgery, and a long recovery. Fortunately I survived the operation and the recovery which involve having a stoma for about a year. 

I stay in the operator for about another 4 years. But then an opportunity came up to leave the operator and spend time with my children to parent them full-time. 

During this time I started to develop my own ideas about how the world works, and how we can make it better. Heavily influenced by my experiences, and my reading of books on a wide range of subjects including economics, politics, psychology, and philosophy. But mainly from my own thinking and reasoning. And from looking at how my daughters were growing and developing, and how they were learning about the world. And for me as parent, thinking about the environment that impacts their well-being and development such as parenting, education, nutrition, social environment, air quality, water quality, food quality, and the economy. 

As a papa I enjoyed every moment with my kids. One of the 'games' we would play is where I would be an aeroplane. They would stand on the sofa and have imaginary tickets. I would stretch my arms out, make aeroplane noises, and spin my arms like properllars. I would stamp their imigary tickets, lift them up horizontally in my arms, and give them 'bumpy' flights around the room or garden. The loved this, and so did many their friends...often asking me to give them 'bumpy flights'!

After struggling to deal with the cognative load and complexity of the world and how my kids were growing up in it, I started to think about how I could help them navigate it. I researched many different deciplines and techniques but could not find anything that I felt was comprehensive enough to help them. So at some point I developed the concept of BUMPS! A way to help them understand the world and how to navigate it.
