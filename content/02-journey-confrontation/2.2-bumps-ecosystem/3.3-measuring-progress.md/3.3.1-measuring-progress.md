# Measuring Progress (Beyond GDP)

## The Problem

## Options

This is the final piece of the puzzle. You are effectively proposing a macro-economic replacement for current financial metrics.

Moving from GDP (which measures transaction volume, even if those transactions are harmful) to GMP/GPPTO/GIVE (which measure evolutionary health) aligns perfectly with the book's philosophy. GIVE (Gross Increased Variety of Environment) is particularly brilliant because it incentivizes the expansion of the "Library of Parts."

## The GDP Fallacy: Why measuring transactions ignores the quality of the problem (solving a disaster raises GDP, but it’s a negative vector).

## GMP (Gross Meaningful Problems): Measuring the input. Are we tackling HUMPS that matter, or just manufacturing noise?

## GPO (Gross Positive Outcomes): Measuring the throughput. Calculating the ratio of transformations that result in a Positive Vector versus those that create negative displacement.

## GIVE (Gross Increased Variety of Environment): Measuring the legacy. Did the transformation expand the library of parts and exaptation potential?

Here is a breakdown of the three metrics to keep handy when writing this chapter:

1. GMP (Gross Meaningful Problems)

The Measure of Ambition.

Traditional economics counts every dollar spent as equal. Bumponomics weights activity by "Meaning."

Calculation: The sum of resources allocated to HUMPS (Strategic/Mission-critical problems) vs. LUMPS (Maintenance).

Goal: Shift society from Low-GMP (fixing trivialities) to High-GMP (transforming existential threats).

2. GPPTO (Gross Positive Problem Transformation Outcomes)

The Measure of Efficacy.

Not all solutions are good. Some create immediate negative displacement (e.g., burning coal solves the energy problem but creates the climate HUMP).

Positive means the outcome expanded the vector.

Formula: Total Transformations minus Negative Displacement Events.

3. GIVE (Gross Increased Variety of Environment)

The Measure of Evolution.

This is the ultimate test of the Bumpivore.

Question: After the problem was transformed, does the system have more options, more diverse parts, and more capability for exaptation than before?

Contrast: A monopoly reduces GIVE (less variety). A healthy ecosystem increases GIVE.
